The Lord of Slaughter {To Lao Tzu, not to fear dying and not to fear killing are equally unnatural and antisocial. Who are we to forestall the judgment of heaven or nature, to usurp the role of “the executioner”? “The Lord of Slaughter” is Waley’s grand translation.}

   ![[Texts/老子/老子74#^KR5c0057xtlsx074-1az3]]
When normal, decent people don’t fear death,

![[Texts/老子/老子74#^KR5c0057xtlsx074-1az4]]
how can you use death to frighten them?

![[Texts/老子/老子74#^KR5c0057xtlsx074-1az5]]
Even when they have a normal fear of death,

![[Texts/老子/老子74#^KR5c0057xtlsx074-1az6]]


![[Texts/老子/老子74#^KR5c0057xtlsx074-1az7]]
who of us dare take and kill

![[Texts/老子/老子74#^KR5c0057xtlsx074-1az8]]
the one who doesn’t?

![[Texts/老子/老子74#^KR5c0057xtlsx074-1az9]]
When people are normal and decent and death-fearing, there’s always an executioner.

![[Texts/老子/老子74#^KR5c0057xtlsx074-1az10]]
To take the place of that executioner

![[Texts/老子/老子74#^KR5c0057xtlsx074-1az11]]
is to take the place of the great carpenter.

![[Texts/老子/老子74#^KR5c0057xtlsx074-1az12]]
People who cut the great carpenter’s wood

![[Texts/老子/老子74#^KR5c0057xtlsx074-1az13]]
seldom get off with their hands unhurt.
