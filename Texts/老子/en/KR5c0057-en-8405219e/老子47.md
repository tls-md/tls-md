Looking far {We tend to expect great things from “seeing the world” and “getting experience.” A Roman poet remarked that travelers change their sky but not their soul. Other poets, untraveled and inexperienced, Emily Brontë and Emily Dickinson, prove Lao Tzu’s point: it’s the inner eye that really sees the world.}

   ![[Texts/老子/老子47#^KR5c0057xtlsx047-1az3]]
You don’t have to go out the door

![[Texts/老子/老子47#^KR5c0057xtlsx047-1az4]]
to know what goes on in the world.

![[Texts/老子/老子47#^KR5c0057xtlsx047-1az5]]
You don’t have to look out the window

![[Texts/老子/老子47#^KR5c0057xtlsx047-1az6]]
to see the way of heaven.

![[Texts/老子/老子47#^KR5c0057xtlsx047-1az7]]
The farther you go,

![[Texts/老子/老子47#^KR5c0057xtlsx047-1az8]]
the less you know.

![[Texts/老子/老子47#^KR5c0057xtlsx047-1az9]]
So the wise soul doesn’t go, but knows;

![[Texts/老子/老子47#^KR5c0057xtlsx047-1az10]]
doesn’t look, but sees;

![[Texts/老子/老子47#^KR5c0057xtlsx047-1az11]]
doesn’t do, but gets it done.
